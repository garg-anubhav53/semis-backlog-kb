## QUESTION 3: MOAT ASSESSMENT

### Classification: QUALIFIED / PRODUCT-LEVEL MONOPOLY

The moat case for EnSilica operates at two levels:

**Level 1 — Product-Level Monopoly (per custom ASIC):** Each custom ASIC EnSilica designs is, by definition, the sole possible source for that specific design. There is exactly one wafer mask set, one GDSII, and one production-qualified supply relationship for each part. Once taped out and qualified, the customer's product is permanently dependent on that specific ASIC from that specific supplier unless they fund a complete redesign. This is a structural monopoly at the individual product level.

**Level 2 — Company-Level Qualified Moat:** The question is whether customers can re-engage a different ASIC design house for the *next* product. They can — but the cost is 12–24+ months of design cycle, full NRE payment to a new supplier, and re-qualification at the end-product level. This is the "QUALIFIED" classification: switching requires requalification that takes over 12 months by definition, making the practical switching cost prohibitive for any product with a production lifecycle of less than 5 years.

### Evidence by Category

**1. Custom ASIC Lock-In (structural)**

Once an EnSilica-designed ASIC is designed into a customer's product:
- The customer's hardware engineers have validated the chip's pinout, power consumption, thermal profile, and firmware interface
- The product's PCB layout is routed around this specific chip package
- Any regulatory or safety certifications (automotive ASIL, industrial IEC 61508, satellite EMC) are tied to the assembled product including the specific ASIC
- Switching to a different ASIC would require a full redesign of the hardware, new regulatory certification, and new production qualification — typically 18–36 months and £500K–£5M+ depending on market

This is not speculative. It is intrinsic to the custom ASIC design model and is the reason TSMC, GlobalFoundries, and ASIC design houses maintain customer retention rates above 90% for production programmes.

**2. 7-Year Contract Structure**

The £18M, 7-year design-and-supply contract is a direct moat indicator. The structure commits both parties:
- **Design phase:** EnSilica delivers the ASIC design (NRE revenue)
- **Supply phase:** The customer commits to purchasing chip supply from EnSilica for 7 years

A 7-year supply commitment from a European electromechanical supplier (automotive/industrial) implies:
- The customer's product has a confirmed production lifecycle of at least 7 years
- The customer has accepted the long-term dependency on EnSilica as their ASIC supplier
- There are likely minimum order quantity (MOQ) commitments embedded in the supply terms
- The customer would face substantial cost and time penalty to exit this relationship mid-term

This is the single most explicit moat indicator in the available data.

**3. £75M Automotive Supply Contract**

Automotive semiconductor supply contracts of this magnitude are issued by Tier-1 suppliers against specific OEM vehicle programmes. Key implications:
- IATF 16949 quality system qualification and AEC-Q100 device qualification are prerequisites for any automotive supply relationship. These are 12–24 month processes.
- Once qualified for a vehicle programme, ENSI is the sole-source supplier for the lifetime of that programme (typically 5–10 years for the production vehicle, plus 7–15 years of spare parts obligation)
- Vehicle programme-level qualification is product-specific: requalification would require a new ASIC, new AEC-Q100 testing, and Tier-1/OEM approval — easily 2–3 years and millions in cost

**4. ARM Partner Status**

EnSilica is listed as an ARM partner. This accreditation allows EnSilica to incorporate ARM IP cores (Cortex-M, Cortex-A, Ethos NPU, etc.) into their ASIC designs under ARM's licensing framework. For customers who want ARM-architecture processors embedded in their custom ASIC — which is common for IoT, automotive MCUs, and AI edge devices — EnSilica's ARM partner status makes them a qualified partner that has been vetted by ARM. Not all ASIC design houses carry this status, which narrows the qualified supplier pool.

**5. Customer Profile Depth**

- **Siemens (industrial):** Industrial automation customers operate under stringent functional safety and long product lifecycle requirements. A Siemens tape-out implies IEC 61508 compatibility and a 10–20 year product support commitment from the ASIC supplier.
- **AST SpaceMobile (satellite):** Satellite hardware qualification is among the most rigorous in commercial electronics. Space-grade components must pass radiation testing, thermal cycling, vibration testing, and long-lead qualification processes. Once qualified, there is zero tolerance for mid-programme supplier substitution.
- **European Tier-1 automotive suppliers:** As analysed under point 3 above.
- **Oriole Networks (AI photonics):** Photonic integrated circuits for AI infrastructure represent an emerging segment where custom ASIC design expertise is scarce. Oriole's dependence on EnSilica's photonics controller ASIC creates a technology-specific lock-in.

**6. Design Pipeline as Future Moat Inventory**

9+ chips currently in the design pipeline represent 9+ future lock-in relationships. Each chip moving from design to production adds another product-level monopoly to EnSilica's revenue base. This is the key structural dynamic: the moat compounds with each new production chip. The 4 chips currently in production will likely be joined by 4–5 more within 18–24 months, implying a step-change in the recurring revenue base.

### Moat Classification Rationale

**QUALIFIED / PRODUCT-LEVEL MONOPOLY**

"QUALIFIED" at the company level because customers can theoretically re-source a future product to a different ASIC design house, but the requalification cost and time (18–36 months minimum) makes mid-programme switching effectively impossible. "PRODUCT-LEVEL MONOPOLY" because each individual ASIC in production has exactly one authorised supplier by design. The combination of 7-year contract terms, automotive qualification requirements, and satellite/space qualification standards means that even the theoretical switching option is unlikely to be exercised in practice.

This is meaningfully stronger than a "COMPETING" or "DUOPOLY" moat. It is not a pure monopoly at the company level because ENSI does not hold a licence or regulatory status that prevents a customer from engaging a different design house for a new product. But for any product in production, ENSI is the exclusive supplier.

## QUESTION 4: ASYMMETRY — WHY IS THIS NOT IN THE PRICE?

**Market cap at £107M implies ~3.9x FY2026 revenue and ~22x FY2026 EBITDA.** At first glance this does not look cheap. The asymmetry argument is structural, not valuation-based: the market is applying a current-year earnings multiple to a company whose revenue model generates an expanding annuity of locked-in chip supply revenue that is not yet fully visible in reported P&L.

### Specific Reasons for Mispricing

1. **AIM Listing = US Invisibility.** ENSI will not appear in SOXX, SMH, XSD, or any US semiconductor ETF. US quant funds running momentum, quality, or value screens on semiconductor names will not find this company. The universe of US investors who can even see this name is tiny — it requires AIM brokerage access and deliberate UK small-cap research capability. Practically no US fund has this combination for a £107M company.

2. **Business Model Mismatch with Sector Classifications.** ENSI does not map cleanly to any standard semiconductor sub-category. It is not a chipmaker (it does not own fabs), not an IP licensor (it does not sell chip designs as IP blocks), not a design tools company (it does not sell EDA software), and not a traditional engineering services firm. Screeners built around standard SIC codes or GICS classifications will miscategorise or exclude it. The consequence is that no automated sector rotation or ETF inclusion process will ever organically capture ENSI.

3. **Revenue Lumpiness Creates False Signals.** The FY2025 -28% revenue decline would have triggered momentum-based screens to exclude ENSI and would have created a negative impression among any investor running a simple revenue growth screen. The recovery in FY2026 (+51%) is now visible but the overhang of the prior year decline likely kept momentum followers out through much of FY2026.

4. **Operational Cash Flow Not Yet Positive.** The cash breakeven is expected by end-2026. Any screen requiring positive FCF or positive operating cash flow excludes ENSI today. This single filter removes ENSI from the majority of quality screens (which is the right place for a screener to exclude it), but it also means that a substantial group of investors who would be natural holders — dividend funds, FCF yield funds, positive earnings quality funds — cannot yet buy it. The inflection is approaching.

5. **Only 1–2 Sell-Side Analysts, No US Coverage.** There is no broker marketing this stock to US institutions. No one is putting ENSI in front of a Fidelity or T. Rowe Price or Wellington portfolio manager. The single analyst providing a price target (130p) is presumably a small UK broker. Without analyst marketing activity, institutional awareness building is essentially zero.

6. **The £250M Contracted Backlog Is Not in Standard Financial Ratios.** A simple P/E or EV/EBITDA screen cannot see the £250M in lifetime supply revenue that is contractually committed but not yet delivered. The market cap of £107M is less than half this contracted revenue figure. Investors running standard valuation screens will not surface this relationship. It requires reading the narrative text of investor presentations and RNS announcements.

7. **No Consensus Narrative.** ENSI is not a "semiconductor AI play" narrative stock (it is not an AI chip designer). It is not a "WFE cycle recovery" story (it has no exposure to equipment). It is not a "Chinese demand" story. The absence of a narrative that connects ENSI to the current consensus theme means no analyst will use it as a thematic pitch to clients, even if they know about it.

## RISKS AND CAVEATS

### Key Risks

1. **Customer Concentration:** The data does not disclose what percentage of revenue comes from the top 1–3 customers. In ASIC design services, customer concentration risk is real — the loss of a major customer's next programme could create another FY2025-type dip.

2. **Cash Position Thin:** £2M cash at H1 FY2026 is thin. If cash flow breakeven slips past end-2026, the company may need to raise equity. AIM companies raising capital at this size typically do so through a small placings at a discount, which would be dilutive. This is the primary financial risk.

3. **Revenue Lumpiness Continues:** Even with 80% of FY2027 pre-contracted, the remaining 20% creates potential for a guidance miss if a major chip delivery is delayed. ASIC tape-outs can be delayed by semiconductor foundry capacity constraints or design iterations.

4. **Foundry Dependency:** EnSilica is fabless — it relies on third-party foundries (likely TSMC, GlobalFoundries, or regional European fabs) for chip production. Supply chain disruptions at foundry level could affect delivery timelines.

5. **Daily Volume Constraint:** Estimated £100K–£300K daily volume means position sizing above £500K–£1M requires multi-week accumulation and would create measurable price impact. This is a structural constraint on position sizing for any institutional buyer.

6. **FY2026 EBITDA Not Adjusted:** The £4.7M EBITDA figure has not been independently verified for IFRS 16 lease adjustments or share-based compensation normalisation from this analysis.
